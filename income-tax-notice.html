Recent Engagement — How We Helped

Context: a salaried professional who received a demand notice under Section 143(1) for Rs 3.2 lakhs due to mismatch between Form 26AS and ITR.

Challenge: The assessee had claimed TDS credit for Rs 4.8 lakhs but Form 26AS showed only Rs 1.6 lakhs. The shortfall resulted in an automatic demand with interest under Section 234B/C. The individual was confused and worried about potential prosecution.

Our approach: We obtained TDS certificates from the employer and verified them against Form 26AS. We filed an online rectification request under Section 154 with supporting TDS certificates and the employer's revised 26Q acknowledgment.

Outcome: The demand was fully withdrawn within 45 days of rectification filing. No interest or penalty applied. We also helped the assessee set up a system to verify 26AS reconciliation before filing future returns.

When Is Income Tax Notice Reply Not Required?

Professional response to income tax notice may not be required when: (a) the notice is a routine intimation under Section 143(1) with a small adjustment that can be accepted, (b) the notice requires only basic information that you can provide without professional computation, (c) the notice is a reminder for return filing resolvable by filing the pending return, or (d) the demand is clearly erroneous and can be resolved through the online rectification facility. Notices under Sections 148 (reassessment), 143(2) (scrutiny), and 263 (revision) always warrant professional representation.

Frequently Asked Questions

I received a Section 143(1) intimation. Is this a notice?

Section 143(1) is an automated processing intimation, not a notice. It shows the tax department computed result vs. your filed return. If there is a demand, you can either accept (pay) or file a rectification request (Section 154) if you disagree.

What is a Section 148 notice?

A reassessment notice — the AO believes income has escaped assessment. You must file a return in response. We evaluate the validity of the notice (was proper procedure followed?) and draft the response/return accordingly.

How quickly must I respond to an IT notice?

Varies by notice type. Section 142(1): typically 15-30 days. Section 143(2): as specified (usually 15-21 days). Section 148: 30 days to file return. Always respond within the deadline — non-response can lead to ex-parte assessment.

What types of IT notices can I receive?

Section 142(1): information/return filing. Section 143(2): scrutiny. Section 148/148A: reassessment. Section 156: demand. Section 245: set-off of refund. Each has different response timeline and implications.

What is the time limit to respond to IT notice?

Section 142(1): 15 days (extendable). Section 143(2): 30 days. Section 148A: 7-30 days. Non-response: assessment completed on best judgment basis, penalties imposed. Always respond within time even if requesting extension.

Can I respond to IT notices myself?

You can, but not recommended for 143(2) scrutiny or 148 reassessment. AO questions require strategic responses — admitting one point may create liability on another. Professional representation protects against self-incriminating responses.

What is faceless assessment?

Since 2020, most IT assessments are faceless (no physical meetings). National Faceless Assessment Centre issues notices and orders electronically. Response filed on e-portal. Video conferencing for hearings. Reduces corruption but requires precise written responses.

What happens if I ignore an IT notice?

Section 144: best judgment assessment (AO estimates income at high levels). Section 271(1)(b): penalty of ₹10,000 for non-compliance. Prosecution possible under Section 276D. Tax demand created without your input. Never ignore.
